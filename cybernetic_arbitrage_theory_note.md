# Beyond The Coasean Singularity

## Context, Leakage, and the Cybernetic Rollup

### Abstract

A growing AI narrative holds that sufficiently capable agents will drive transaction costs toward zero and dissolve more economic activity from firms into markets. A broader version of the same story imagines billions of agentic "daemons" continuously transacting on behalf of users and firms, producing a more fluid and programmable economy. This note argues that both views are incomplete. Lower search, bargaining, and monitoring costs do matter, but they do not resolve a more important bundle of frictions: context legibility, context leakage, trust-dependent context generation, actuator access, and liability-bearing control. In many economically important settings, valuable context is not merely hidden; it is constituted in trusted interaction at the edge. External coordination therefore requires revealing information that both improves matching and erodes rents. Ownership changes this tradeoff by altering the information topology of coordination: who can see what, share what, act on what, and learn from what. The likely institutional consequence is not a fully marketized daemon economy, but the rise of edge routers and cybernetic rollups that own the nodes where context is born and can internalize the gains from acting on it.

## 1. The question

The current argument around AI-mediated coordination usually begins with Coase. If firms exist because markets are costly to use, then AI agents that lower search, negotiation, monitoring, and contracting costs should reduce the scope of firms. The stronger version of this claim is a daemon world: billions of software agents continually transacting and coordinating on behalf of principals, causing more of the economy to clear through dynamic markets rather than through hierarchy.

This note argues that the transaction-cost story is too thin to explain the institutional form of an AI economy. A better question is not simply whether AI lowers coordination cost, but whether it changes the value of ownership when valuable context is local, leakage-prone, trust-sensitive, and only partially actionable without control.

## 2. What the transaction-cost story gets right

The Coasean-singularity thesis is directionally right on several points.

- AI can reduce search and matching cost.
- AI can lower drafting, verification, and monitoring cost in some transactions.
- Some functions currently performed inside firms may become more modular and more market-like.
- Mechanism design opportunities should expand when more information can be processed cheaply.

These are real effects. The mistake is treating them as sufficient to determine the future boundary of the firm.

## 3. What the story misses

The missing variables are not peripheral. They are often the central source of economic value.

### 3.1 Context legibility

Economic coordination is bounded by what can be made legible. In real settings, relevant context is dispersed, tacit, rapidly changing, and embedded in operational detail. Lower bargaining cost does not imply that the right information becomes visible at the right time to the right counterparty.

### 3.2 Context leakage

Revealing context is not free. To coordinate well with an external counterparty, a firm often has to reveal demand, urgency, capacity, quality problems, risk tolerance, workflow state, or customer intent. That revelation may improve the immediate match while reducing future rents through price discrimination, competitive imitation, or strategic dependence. The coordination problem is therefore not just a cost problem; it is a leakage problem.

### 3.3 Trust-dependent context generation

Some of the most valuable context does not exist in a transferable form before interaction. It is generated by virtue of a trusted interface with a user or operator. A doctor's real-time clinical uncertainty, an underwriter's edge-case concern, or a field operator's exception judgment may only become legible when the actor trusts the system enough to reveal it naturally. In such cases, the context is not merely hidden from a centralized router. It is not yet created.

### 3.4 Actuator access

Coordination value depends not just on seeing context, but on being able to act on it. Non-owning middleware can often recommend, route, or suggest. Owning systems can reallocate labor, reprioritize inventory, alter workflows, absorb risk, and enforce execution. The economic gap between recommendation and control is large.

### 3.5 Liability-bearing control

In many domains, automation without liability is advisory theater. The party that bears downside risk captures a different set of rights over process, evidence, and operational change than the party that merely sells cognition. Liability and control are therefore not afterthoughts to coordination; they define who can operationalize intelligence.

### 3.6 Cumulative learning

Repeated coordination inside an owned system can generate a compounding advantage. Shared outcomes improve policies, routing, exception handling, and deployment playbooks. This learning can happen in markets too, but internal ownership changes what data is retained, who can combine it, and whether the resulting advantage leaks.

## 4. Why cheap agents do not automatically produce markets

This view is consistent with early empirical signals. When AI agents are placed into simulated bargaining or internal-market settings, they do not obviously generate thick, efficient, voluntary markets. They often default to passivity, autarky, norm-conforming fairness, or brittle mechanism-dependence. This matters because it suggests that lowering explicit transaction costs does not dissolve the need for institutional structure. It may instead shift the terms on which structure is designed.

The key point is not that agents will fail. It is that the hard part of coordination is not exhausted by bargain-finding. Even with capable agents, the economy may continue to favor structures that internalize high-value context and reduce leakage.

## 5. From routers to rollups

If the critical scarcity is context rather than pure cognition, then the best coordinating entity is not necessarily a generic middleware platform. It is an entity that can do four things at once:

- originate or own trusted sensors
- internalize the context generated by those sensors
- route over that context
- act on the resulting decisions through owned or controlled actuators

An edge router is a first step. It sits where context is born and can make a previously illegible domain legible enough to match users to solutions. But the stronger form is a cybernetic rollup: an entity that owns or tightly controls the operational nodes, captures the context they generate, and compounds that context through repeated action and learning.

The rollup is not just a larger firm. It is a different information structure. Owning adjacent nodes changes who can share state internally, how much context leaks externally, and whether success in one node improves policy in another. This is why the value of a portfolio of assets may be more than additive under AI.

## 6. A different law of profit migration

If intelligence becomes more modular and cheap, attractive profits do not necessarily accrue to cognition resellers. They can migrate toward the layer that owns trust, context, and actuator access. In that sense AI may invert familiar aggregation intuitions. Instead of winning by modularizing supply without operating it, the next winners may capture value by internalizing the unmodularizable parts of coordination.

That does not mean all markets disappear. It means the highest-margin or most defensible positions may emerge where context is born and where acting on context does not require disclosing it to someone else.

## 7. Propositions

The cybernetic-arbitrage thesis can be stated as a set of falsifiable propositions.

### Proposition 1

Reducing search and bargaining cost alone does not imply thick decentralized coordination.

### Proposition 2

When coordination requires revealing local context, leakage costs can offset or exceed transaction-cost savings.

### Proposition 3

Some high-value economic context is trust-generated rather than merely pre-existing, and therefore cannot be fully intermediated by untrusted external agents.

### Proposition 4

Owning adjacent nodes increases surplus capture more than proportionally when shared context improves routing, execution, and learning.

### Proposition 5

Actuator access and liability-bearing control matter as much as information access in capturing the gains from AI coordination.

## 8. Implications for simulation

If these propositions are right, then a useful simulation should not model the economy as a graph where lower transaction costs automatically thicken markets. It should model:

- local private state at each node
- transactions that require partial revelation of that state
- a tradeoff between better matching and information leakage
- trust-sensitive context generation
- varying degrees of actuator access
- ownership structures that change what information can be shared internally

The relevant comparison is not just market versus firm. It is at least three worlds:

- fragmented market coordination
- external non-owning router
- owning cybernetic rollup

The outputs should include not just welfare or gross surplus, but leakage-adjusted surplus, routing quality, learning rate, and the marginal value of acquiring adjacent nodes. The central question is whether ownership changes the economics of coordination by changing the information topology of the system.

## 9. Implications for the fund

This matters for capital allocation. A fund should not only ask where AI can compress labor or reduce cycle time. It should also ask where ownership creates access to proprietary, reusable, high-value context. The best assets are not simply the most automatable. They are the ones where AI application and context aggregation reinforce each other.

Under that view, the event log is more than an instrumentation layer. It is a context-accounting system: the substrate that makes edge-born context durable, queryable, and reusable across a portfolio.

## 10. Next step

The next step is to formalize these propositions in a simulation that can compare decentralized agentic coordination against edge routers and cybernetic rollups under realistic assumptions about leakage, trust, and actuator control. That simulation should be treated as a test of institutional form, not merely a demonstration that AI agents can trade.
